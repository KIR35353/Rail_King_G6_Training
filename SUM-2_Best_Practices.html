Operational Best Practices
📋
Log Every Shift — Even Uneventful Ones
A "nothing to report" entry with unit ID, hour meter, and operator name is a valid and valuable record. It establishes baseline condition and supports warranty coverage.
DOCUMENTATION
🌡
Act on Trend, Not on Alarm
Reduce load when a gauge climbs toward the upper band — before the lamp illuminates. Proactive response avoids forced shutdowns and protects components from threshold damage.
MONITORING
⚙
Warm Up and Cool Down Are Not Optional
Low-idle warm-up before loading and low-idle cool-down before shutdown protect the turbocharger, hydraulic seals, and engine bearings — the most expensive components on the machine.
OPERATION
🔍
Post-Op Is the Best Time to Find Faults
A warm machine shows its leaks. An end-of-shift inspection costs 5 minutes; a mid-shift breakdown costs hours and risks safety. Inspect while you still have time to act in an orderly way.
INSPECTION
🤝
Accept the Machine — Accept the Responsibility
Never sign a handoff log without completing your own walk-around. The incoming operator who skips the check is the operator who inherits an undiscovered fault.
HANDOFF
On the Job — Always and Never
These are the hard rules that do not bend under time pressure or convenience.
✓ Always
✓Complete the walk-around before every startup — no exceptions.
✓Lower all loads before shutdown or emergency stop.
✓Record fault codes exactly as displayed — don't rely on memory.
✓Match the model to the manual before applying any specification.
✓Escalate when the fault is beyond your authority to resolve.
✗ Never
✗Clear a red fault lamp and restart without identifying the cause.
✗Leave the ignition key in an unattended machine.
✗Park with a load raised — pressure bleeds after shutdown.
✗Apply a service-level adjustment without the correct document in hand.
✗Accept a machine handoff without performing your own check.
