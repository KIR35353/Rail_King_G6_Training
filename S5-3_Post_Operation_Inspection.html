Post-Operation Inspection
Section 5
1
Walk the Exterior — Structural and Visible Damage
Check frame, body panels, guards, and attachment mounting points. Look for cracks, bent components, missing fasteners, or impact damage not noticed during operation.
VISUAL
360° walk
2
Check for Fluid Leaks
Inspect ground beneath the machine and all circuit connections. Check engine oil, coolant, hydraulic lines, gearbox breathers, and air fittings for seepage, drips, or puddles.
FLUIDS
ground check
3
Fluid Level Top-Ups
Top up engine oil, coolant, hydraulic reservoir, and diesel DEF as required. Record any quantity added in the shift log — unusual consumption indicates a developing fault.
TOP-UP
log qty
4
Tyre / Track and Undercarriage Check
Check tyre pressures visually (or with gauge per site procedure). Inspect tracks for tension, damaged pads, or missing hardware. Clear any debris packed into undercarriage.
RUNNING GEAR
clear debris
5
Attachment Condition and Security
Check cutting edges, wear plates, pins, and retaining hardware. Any excessive wear, cracking, or loose pins must be documented and flagged to service before next shift use.
ATTACHMENT
wear / pins
6
Complete Post-Op Log Entry and Defect Report
Record final hour meter, any defects found during post-op, fluid quantities added, and machine condition on handover. Any defect that affects safety or operability must be formally reported.
LOG + REPORT
before leaving
Why Post-Op Matters
The post-op inspection is the best time to find faults — the machine is warm and any leaks or loose hardware that developed during operation are visible. A defect found at shutdown is an orderly repair; the same defect found mid-shift next day becomes a breakdown and potential safety event.
Defect Severity Classification
Red — Out of service: Fluid loss, brake fault, structural crack, attachment pin missing
Amber — Monitor / plan repair: Seep (not a drip), minor wear, non-critical lamp
Green — Serviceable: Logged, no immediate impact on safety or operation
Unusual Fluid Consumption
Any fluid consumption greater than normal should be logged with quantity. A machine that requires 2 L of hydraulic oil top-up every shift has a leak — finding it early prevents a major failure and environmental contamination incident.
The post-op inspection closes the shift. No operator leaves a machine without completing the log entry and filing any defect report — even if the next operator will be on site immediately.
